Theory

The spin coherence time T₂ describes the "quantum lifetime" of a qubit spin state in the presence of environmental noise. Interactions between the qubit spin and its environment can be described by the following many-body Hamiltonian:

\[ \hat{H} = \hat{H}_{\mathrm{qubit}} + \hat{H}_{\mathrm{bath}} + \hat{H}_{\mathrm{qubit\text{-}bath}} = \mathbf{SDS} + \mathbf{B} \gamma_e \mathbf{S} + \sum_i \mathbf{S} \mathbf{A}_i \mathbf{I}_i + \mathbf{B} \gamma_i \mathbf{I}_i + \sum_{i>j} \mathbf{I}_i \mathbf{J}_{ij} \mathbf{I}_j \]

where \(\hat{S}_z\) is ... From \(\hat{H}\), the coherence function \(L(t)\) of the qubit can be computed

\[ L(t) = \frac{ \langle 1 | \rho(t) | 0 \rangle}{\langle 1 | \rho(0) | 0 \rangle } \]

Then, T₂ can be computed by fitting \(L(t)\) to a stretched exponential form:

\[ L(t) = \exp\left(\left( \frac{t}{T_2} \right)^{\eta}\right) \]

In principle, the Methods to rapidly predict T₂ have been developed in Refs. [1][2]. In developing these methods, several assumptions are made:

  • The dominant decoherence mechanism is magnetic dipolar interactions between the qubit spin and nuclear isotopes present in the material. As a result, T₂ is largely a property of the host material, rather than the spin defect itself.
  • The secular approximation is invoked, which is permitted when \(\hat{S}_z\) is a good quantum number. This is generally a safe approximation in the high magnetic field limit.
  • The stretch exponent \(\eta\) is set to \(\eta = 2\) for 3D systems [1] and \(\eta = 2.84\) for 2D systems [2].

For each naturally abundant isotope of every element in the structure, the nuclear spin density \(n\) is computed from the lattice volume and natural abundance. The single-element coherence time scales as [1]:

\[ T_2^{(\alpha)} \propto \left|g_\alpha\right|^{-1.64} \, I_\alpha^{-1.1} \, n_\alpha^{-1} \]

where \(g_\alpha\) is the nuclear g-factor, \(I_\alpha\) the nuclear spin quantum number, and \(n_\alpha\) the nuclear spin density of isotope \(\alpha\). Contributions from all isotopes are combined using the Kanai power-law formula [1]:

\[ \frac{1}{T_2^{3/\eta}} = \sum_\alpha \frac{1}{\left(T_2^{(\alpha)}\right)^{3/\eta}} \]

For 2D materials, a geometric correction factor is applied to account for the reduced dimensionality of the nuclear spin bath [2]. The material thickness \(d\) is estimated from the van der Waals radii of the outermost atoms, and the 2D nuclear spin density is defined as:

\[ n^{\mathrm{2D}}_\alpha = n^{\mathrm{3D}}_\alpha \cdot d \]
